The Prisoner's Dilemma

Imagine that there are two prisoners both convicted for a joint crime. The prosecutor has insufficient evidence to build a case against either of the prisoners, and so offers each of them a deal. The prisoners are being held in separate rooms and so can’t communicate.

The prosecutor tells each prisoner that if they confess to the crime, providing enough evidence to convict the other prisoner, then they will walk free but leaving their fellow convict to 10 years in prison. However, if both the prisoners choose to confess then they will both have to serve 7 years in prison. If they instead both remain silent, they will be forced to serve a year before they are released.

Now lets define ‘rational’ as acting in a way that will result in the shortest sentence in jail. By this definition the rational decision in this situation is always to confess. This is because if the other prisoner remains silent then the optimum strategy is to confess and walk free, the other case is that the other prisoner confesses and again in this case the best strategy is to confess and serve 7 years.

If both prisoners act rationally and choose to confess then they will end up with a significantly worse outcome than if they cooperated and both remained silent. This is the prisoner’s dilemma.

The prisoner’s dilemma is a standard example of a game in that shows why rational individuals may not cooperate.

Evolutionary Game Theory

Evolutionary game theory applies classical game theory to evolution. It is funda- mentally different from classical game theory in the respect that, instead of consid- ering contests between rational decision makers, we suppose that each member of a population assumes a behaviour strategy, then the success of a certain strategy will depend on contests between members of the population.

Lets define a behaviour strategy as an individual having a predisposition to act in a certain way. It is important to consider the individual as not being consciously aware of the strategy they have adopted, then this provides a way of modelling evolution at the “phenotypic level”. A contest is an interaction between two individuals within the population that provokes them to act in a manner that is dictated by an individuals behavioural phenotype, which we will refer to as an individuals strategy.

We can expand the example of the Prisoner’s dilemma into the framework of evolutionary game theory we have outline. In this context the Prisoner’s dilemma provides an interesting metaphor for cooperation in a population. Now instead of having two rational players in a contest, we define there to be a population where individuals can belong to 2 opposing strategies. The first strategy, C, is defined as individuals who will either cooperate and remain silent. The second strategy, D, which we will define as individuals who will defect and confess their crime.

Cellular Automata

A cellular automaton is an n-dimensional array containing individual cells that an take a finite number of discrete values. There are a given set of rules dictating what value a cell will take after a discrete time period. These rules are dependent only on a cell’s immediate neighbours.

How we wish to define a cell's immediate neighbours is up to us. Two commonly used definitions for a cells neighbourhood is Von nuemann neighbourhood and a Moore's neighbourhood
